There are more "trading strategies" floating around the internet than there are profitable traders. The signal-to-noise ratio is brutal.

Strip away the marketing and almost every working approach falls into one of eight buckets. Each one has a regime where it prints money and a regime where it gets shredded. The skill isn't picking the "best" one — **the skill is knowing which one fits the market you're actually in, and being honest when conditions change.**

Here's the field, with the math and the failure mode for each.

---

## 1. Trend following

**The idea:** When price is moving in one direction with conviction, get in and stay in until momentum dies.

**Typical setup:**

* Higher highs and higher lows on multiple timeframes
* Pullback to a moving average (20 or 50 EMA) holds
* Entry on the bounce, stop below the swing low

**The math:**

* Win rate: 30–45%
* Reward-to-risk: 2:1 to 5:1
* Most trades lose; the few winners pay for everything

> ***Trend following pays you for being right about direction, not frequency.***

**Where it works:** Strong directional markets — early bull runs, major commodity moves, momentum-driven equities post-breakout.

**Where it breaks:** Choppy, range-bound markets. You get death by a thousand small losses while waiting for a real trend. **If you can't tolerate a 40% drawdown in your strategy equity curve, you can't trade trend.**

---

## 2. Mean reversion

**The idea:** Prices overshoot. After an extreme move, they tend to come back to an average.

**Typical setup:**

* Price stretched 2+ standard deviations from a moving average
* Oversold on RSI (sub-30) or overbought (above 70)
* Entry on the snapback, exit at the mean

**The math:**

* Win rate: 60–75%
* Reward-to-risk: 0.5:1 to 1:1
* High frequency of small wins, occasional large losses

**Where it works:** Range-bound markets, index ETFs, large-cap stocks during periods of low realized volatility.

**Where it breaks:** Strong trends. The biggest mean-reversion losses come from a "snapback that never happens" — price keeps going, your stop hits, and the asymmetric loss erases dozens of small wins.

> ***Mean reversion is selling insurance. The premium feels free until the day it isn't.***

---

## 3. Breakout trading

**The idea:** When price clears a key level on volume, momentum continues in that direction.

**Typical setup:**

* Multi-week consolidation pattern (flag, range, triangle)
* Volume contracts during the base, expands on the break
* Entry on the breakout candle, stop back inside the range

**The math:**

* Win rate: 35–50%
* Reward-to-risk: 2:1 to 4:1
* False breakouts are common; the real ones move far

**Where it works:** Volatile markets with strong catalysts — earnings, macro events, sector rotations. Works best when implied volatility is elevated and IV-rank is rising.

**Where it breaks:** Low-volatility, news-poor environments. You get fakeout after fakeout — price clears the level, you size in, and it immediately reverses. The fix: require a confirmation close, not just a wick through.

---

## 4. Momentum trading

**The idea:** Strong moves attract more buyers, which makes them stronger. Ride the self-reinforcing loop.

**Typical setup:**

* Stock up 5%+ on the day with a catalyst (earnings, news, sector flow)
* Relative volume 3x+ normal
* Price holds the VWAP on pullback
* Entry on the bounce off VWAP, trail stop higher as it runs

**The math:**

* Win rate: 40–55%
* Holding period: hours to days
* Killers: gap fills, catalyst-fade reversals

**Where it works:** Small-cap movers, post-earnings drift in large caps, sector rotations.

> ***Momentum doesn't care about valuation. It cares about flow.***

**Where it breaks:** Late-cycle momentum trades — when retail piles in on day 3 of a parabolic move, the next move is usually a 30% gap down. Get in early or don't get in.

---

## 5. Scalping

**The idea:** Take many small wins per session, hold each trade for seconds to minutes.

**Typical setup:**

* Liquid product (ES futures, SPY, large-cap names)
* Order-flow signal: bid/ask imbalance, absorption at a level, large prints
* Target: 1–3 ticks. Stop: 1–2 ticks.

**The math:**

* Win rate: 55–70%
* Reward-to-risk: ~1:1
* Edge comes from frequency and execution, not idea quality

**Where it works:** Liquid markets with persistent two-sided flow. Best during the regular session open, FOMC, and earnings season.

**Where it breaks:** Anywhere with bad fills, wide spreads, or thin liquidity. Commissions and slippage will eat your edge before you even know it's gone. **Scalping is the strategy where execution costs are the strategy.**

---

## 6. Swing trading

**The idea:** Hold positions for days to weeks to capture multi-day moves. The catch-all bucket between intraday and investing.

**Typical setup:**

* Daily-chart setup (e.g., pullback to 20 EMA in an uptrend)
* Entry on a confirmation candle
* Stop below the swing low; target at 2–3x risk or next resistance
* Hold through overnight risk

**The math:**

* Win rate: 45–55%
* Reward-to-risk: 1.5:1 to 3:1
* Position sizing is critical — overnight gaps can blow through stops

**Where it works:** Trending markets with clean structure. Forgiving of execution skill, demanding of patience.

**Where it breaks:** Earnings season for single names — held overnight, a -8% earnings gap blows past your stop. Rule: **never hold a single-name swing through earnings unless that's the trade.**

---

## 7. Pairs trading / statistical arbitrage

**The idea:** Two historically correlated assets diverge. Long the laggard, short the leader, and bet the spread reverts.

**Typical setup:**

* Two assets with strong historical correlation (e.g., KO and PEP, GLD and SLV)
* Spread is 2+ standard deviations from its mean
* Entry: long the underperformer, short the outperformer
* Exit: when spread closes back to the mean

**The math:**

* Win rate: 65–80%
* Returns per trade: small (often <1%)
* Market neutral — works in up, down, and sideways tape
* Killers: correlation regime change (the pair stops being a pair)

**Where it works:** Stable correlation regimes. Quant funds run this at scale because the small edge compounds when you can put on hundreds of pairs.

> ***Pairs trading is profitable until the relationship breaks. Then it's catastrophic.***

**Where it breaks:** When one of the underlying businesses has a fundamental shift (M&A, accounting fraud, regulatory action). The "spread reverts to the mean" assumption fails when the mean itself moves.

---

## 8. Options income — the wheel and credit spreads

**The idea:** Sell premium on assets you'd be willing to own. Collect time decay and implied volatility risk premium.

**The wheel:**

1. Sell cash-secured puts on stocks you want to own at lower prices
2. If assigned, you now own the stock at a discount
3. Sell covered calls against the position
4. If called away, restart at step 1

**Credit spreads:** Sell an option, buy a further OTM option to cap risk. Collect the difference as premium.

**The math:**

* Win rate: 70–85%
* Reward-to-risk: 0.3:1 to 0.5:1
* You win small, often. You lose big, rarely. **The losses are the trade.**

**Where it works:** High implied-volatility environments, range-bound markets, names you have a thesis on.

**Where it breaks:** Tail events. Selling puts on a "stable" name the week before it gaps -30%. Selling iron condors into FOMC. **Premium sellers get paid until they don't, and the day they don't usually erases a year of income.**

The discipline: position size assuming you take maximum loss on every trade. If that risk is unacceptable, the size is wrong.

## How to actually pick one

Don't pick by which one looks coolest. Pick by which one fits **three things you can be honest about**:

### 1. Your personality

* Can you stare at the screen for 6 hours straight? → scalping or intraday momentum
* Can you wait three weeks for a setup? → swing or trend
* Do losses make you want to revenge trade? → mean reversion will destroy you

### 2. Your capital

* Under $25k → swing or longer-timeframe (PDT rule)
* $25k–$100k → most intraday strategies viable
* $100k+ → pairs, options income, multi-strategy

### 3. The current regime

| Market condition | Strategies that work | Strategies that break |
|---|---|---|
| Strong trend, low vol | Trend following, momentum | Mean reversion |
| Choppy range, high vol | Mean reversion, premium selling | Trend, breakouts |
| News-driven, high vol | Breakouts, gap-and-go, momentum | Mean reversion, premium selling |
| Low-volume drift | Pairs trading, swing | Scalping, breakouts |

The trader who only knows one strategy is going to spend half their career losing money. The trader who knows when to **stop trading their preferred strategy** is the one who survives.

## The honest summary

There is no holy grail. Every strategy on this list has:

* a regime where it makes money
* a regime where it loses money
* a defined math behind both
* a known failure mode

> ***The strategy is not the edge. The discipline to execute it correctly — and stop executing it when the regime changes — is the edge.***

Pick one. Trade it for 100 setups. Journal every trade. Compute your actual win rate and average R. Compare to the published math above.

If your numbers are close, you have a strategy.
If they're not, you have a hobby.

The market doesn't care which one it is. You should.
