Most traders don't fail because they can't read a chart. They fail because they make the same handful of mistakes over and over until the account is gone.

Studies of brokerage data are unambiguous: **the most active retail traders consistently underperform both the market and less active investors**. Trading psychology is responsible for roughly 80% of trading losses — not bad signals, not bad luck, not bad markets. Behavior.

Here are the ten mistakes that do most of the damage.

---

## 1. Trading without a stop loss

The single most expensive habit in retail trading.

A planned -1% loss is a feature of a working system. An unplanned -25% drawdown because you "wanted to give it room to work" is how accounts die. Every position you take must have a stop *before* you click buy. If you can't define where you're wrong, you don't have a trade — you have a wish.

> ***Removing or widening a stop after entry is not flexibility. It's the moment your edge dies.***

---

## 2. Revenge trading after a loss

You take a loss. You're angry. You jump back in larger — to "make it back."

That's how a -1% day turns into a -8% day, and how a -8% day turns into a blown account. Revenge trading is the **#1 cause of failed prop firm evaluations**. It's not the loss that kills you. It's the emotional response to the loss.

Rule: after two consecutive losses, walk away from the screen for an hour. After three, you're done for the day.

---

## 3. Overtrading

Death by a thousand cuts.

The trader who takes 30 setups a day on a small account is not "more active" — they're paying commissions and spread on every trade while their concentration deteriorates. Data shows **overtraders experience roughly 25% higher loss rates** than traders who stick to a defined plan.

The fix isn't motivation. It's a hard cap: max N trades per session, no exceptions.

---

## 4. The disposition effect — cutting winners, holding losers

Traders are **1.5x more likely to sell a winner than a loser**. The disposition effect alone reduces annual returns by 3–5%.

The psychology is simple and brutal:

* Selling a winner *feels* like locking in skill
* Selling a loser *feels* like admitting failure

So you take +0.8R and let -3R run. Do that enough times and the math destroys you regardless of win rate.

> ***Your job is not to be right. Your job is to make the loss small and let the right ones run.***

---

## 5. Position sizing by feel

"This setup looks really good, I'll size up."

This is where the account actually dies — not on the bad trades, but on the oversized "high conviction" ones. A 5% risk per trade means **roughly 14 consecutive losses ruin you**. A 1% risk gives you ~70.

Same edge. Same signals. The only thing that changed is whether you survive long enough for the edge to play out.

See: [Position Sizing: The Only Edge That Actually Compounds](/posts/position-sizing-the-only-edge-that-actually-compounds/)

---

## 6. Confirmation bias

You take a long. Then you go looking for bullish news, bullish chartists on Twitter, bullish indicators.

You are no longer trading the market. You are trading your thesis. Every disconfirming signal gets dismissed; every confirming signal gets weighted 3x.

The discipline: **before you enter, write down what would prove you wrong**. If that condition appears, exit — no negotiation, no rationalization.

---

## 7. Recency bias

The last three trades were winners → next setup looks better than it is → you size up.

The last three trades were losers → next setup looks worse than it is → you skip a perfectly valid signal.

Your brain is wired to overweight recent events. Your trading plan exists to override that wiring. **The setup is the setup**, regardless of what happened in the previous hour.

---

## 8. No journal, no review

If you don't write it down, you will repeat it.

Most traders can't tell you:
- their actual win rate over the last 50 trades
- their average R per win vs. average R per loss
- which setup type makes them money and which loses
- what time of day they're most profitable

Without that data you're not trading — you're gambling with a chart in front of you. A trading journal isn't optional. It's the only feedback loop that closes the gap between what you *think* you're doing and what you're actually doing.

---

## 9. Trading too large for your account size

Tied to position sizing, but a distinct mistake.

Trading futures on a $5k account. Selling naked options with $10k buying power. Sizing in a single trade that represents 40% of equity.

The math doesn't work. **One bad print and you're done.** No amount of skill survives undercapitalization.

If you can't risk less than 1% per trade and still have a meaningful dollar outcome, your account is too small for that instrument. Trade something else, or save more capital first.

---

## 10. Trading without an edge

This one is rarely discussed because it's uncomfortable.

Most retail traders have **no edge** — no statistically validated reason to believe their setups produce positive expectancy. They have:

* a few patterns they read in a book
* some indicators they like the look of
* a vague sense of "where price should go"

That's not a strategy. That's pattern-matching wrapped in confidence.

> ***If you can't describe your edge in one sentence and back it up with at least 100 trades of data, your edge does not exist.***

You need: a defined setup, a defined entry, a defined exit, a defined invalidation, and statistics that show positive expectancy over a meaningful sample. Anything less is hope.

---

## The pattern behind all of them

Every mistake on this list has the same root: **letting emotion override process.**

* Stops get widened because losing feels bad.
* Sizes get bigger because winning feels good.
* Winners get cut because uncertainty feels worse than certainty.
* Losers get held because admitting failure feels worse than waiting.
* Trades get taken because boredom feels worse than activity.

The traders who survive are not smarter. They are not better at reading charts. They are better at **doing the same correct thing whether they feel like it or not.**

That is the entire game.

---

## What to do this week

- [ ] **Write down your invalidation rule** — what would prove your last trade thesis wrong?
- [ ] **Set a daily max-loss limit** in dollars. Hit it, you're done.
- [ ] **Set a daily max-trade count.** Hit it, you're done.
- [ ] **Open a journal**. Log every trade: setup, size, R risked, R outcome, emotional state on entry.
- [ ] **Review last 20 trades**. Find one mistake from this list. Fix only that one before tackling another.

The goal is not to be a great trader this week. The goal is to **stop being a bad one**.

Everything else comes after that.
